Effects of Trip Characteristics on Duration¶

by Olga Gabdulkhakova¶

Investigation Overview¶

In this investigation, I wanted to look at the characteristics of trips that could be used to predict their duration. The main focus was on user type, rental access method, day of the week, and stations.

Dataset Overview¶

There are 295,853 records in the dataset with 8 features: duration, start time, end time, start station name, end station name, user type, rental access method, and day of the week. Duration is the only quantitative variable, the rest are qualitative variables. Despite a considerable number of outliers, only one presumably faulty record has been removed from the dataset.

Distribution of trip durations¶

Duration variable proved to have an extremely long tail, with the majority of data lying before 3,000 seconds (50 minutes). The outliers are 4527 records above 3,000 seconds (50 minutes) and below 86,222 seconds (23.95 hours) in duration.

Relationships Between the Three Categorical Features¶

There doesn't seem to be that much interaction between the rental access method and user type and day of the week, though proportionally it seems like customers are more likely to use clippers than apps. It also seems that proportionally a larger number of customers take rides on weekends. We can see that Saturdays and Sundays have the closest ratio between subscribers and customers, while Wednesdays, Thursdays and Fridays have a more distant ratio.

Trip Duration for Top 5 Start Stations Across Different Categories¶

Specific stations don't seem to have any real effect on average trip duration in the context of user type and rental access method: customers and app users take longer rides. The interesting observation is that these most popular stations account for proportionally longer trips on weekdays, not weekends. Previously we revealed that weekend trips tend to be longer. This might due to the fact that popular stations are more often used for work commute during the weekdays.
